# Van Gennep vs Eliade — sacred-as-relation vs sacred-as-quality

## The disagreement

Van Gennep treats sacredness as a *relation*. Sacredness is not an
attribute of an object, place, or person; it is brought into play by
the configuration of a particular situation. The same individual is
profane to her household and sacred to the strangers she meets at the
frontier. The "magic circles pivot" as a person moves from one
position in society to another ([[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p030]]).

Eliade — in the work queued for source-read — treats hierophany as a
manifestation of the sacred *as quality*: the sacred reveals itself in
specific objects, places, and times that thereby become sacred *as
such*. The axis mundi, the temple, illud tempus — these are sacred,
not merely sacred-relative-to.

The distinction is not terminological. Van Gennep's sacred is a
field that pivots; Eliade's sacred is a property that descends.

## Why operative

Brand canon working in cosmology mode inherits one of two
metaphysics. If sacred is *relational*, then sacred space, sacred
time, and sacred objects in the brand's world get their charge from
the configuration the customer is in — not from the object's
inherent property. Onboarding moments become liminal because of the
customer's relation to the threshold, not because of any quality
the moment carries on its own. Marketing produces relational sacred
through configurations: the right customer, in the right state, at
the right boundary.

If sacred is a *quality*, the brand authors objects, places, and
times that *are* sacred — the homepage as temple, the ritual moment
as illud tempus, the founding artifact as hierophany. Marketing
produces quality-sacred by elevating the artifact, not the
configuration.

Most marketing-side cosmology defaults reflexively to quality-sacred
because objects are easier to author than configurations. Van
Gennep's discipline is the corrective.

## Pressure-test signature

When canon claims that a moment, place, or artifact in the customer's
journey is sacred: check whether the claim is *relational* (sacred
because of the customer's position relative to it) or *qualitative*
(sacred as such). Both are valid. The check: which one is the canon
actually making, and does the brand's onboarding/ritual structure
deliver that one? A quality-sacred claim with relational delivery
will perform; a relational-sacred claim authored as quality will
under-deliver.

## Resolution direction

Both stand. The choice is dimension-specific:

- mythos-rites and mythos-customer-arc lean van-Gennep — passage
  rituals are relational by their structure
- mythos-cosmology can take either; substrate-aware brands often
  combine (gard-utgard is qualitatively sacred *and* the relation
  pivots when you cross it)
- The wrong default is "all sacred is quality" — Eliade's frame
  applied without van Gennep's corrective produces brittle canon
  that imports cathedral-sacred into onboarding moments and
  underperforms

This tension will sharpen when [[eliade-sacred-and-profane|The Sacred and the Profane]] is
source-read and the Eliade side gains its own anchors.

## Contributions

### From [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage|The Rites of Passage]]

The pivoting-of-sacred move is named at [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p030]]:
"the 'magic circles' pivot, shifting as a person moves from one place
in society to another." Van Gennep makes the relational ontology
explicit and load-bearing — the schema of preliminal-liminal-postliminal
only works because sacredness can pivot under the rite. The
counterparty (Eliade) has not yet been source-read; the disagreement
is named here against Eliade's known position from lineage and
queued-source frontmatter, to be sharpened when his book is read.

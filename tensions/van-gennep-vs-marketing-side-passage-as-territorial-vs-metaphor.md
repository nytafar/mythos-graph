# Van Gennep vs marketing-side — passage as territorial vs metaphor

## The disagreement

Van Gennep insists passage is *not* metaphor. The customer who
crosses from one social state to another crosses an actual
threshold; the territorial passage is the operative substrate, and
all later applications of the schema (pregnancy, initiation,
marriage, funeral) are extensions of an actual spatial passage rather
than analogies of one. "These phrases and events are seldom meant
as 'symbols'; for the semicivilized the passage is actually a
territorial passage" ([[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p377]]). The
schema is grounded in the threshold made of stone, the doorway made
of wood, the neutral zone made of forest.

Marketing-side compressions of the customer journey (StoryBrand,
the hero's-journey-for-marketers compressions of Vogler) take the
schema as pure metaphor. The "customer journey" is a narrative
device; the "threshold" is a section heading; the "transformation"
is a copy beat. The territorial substrate is dropped because the
substrate is treated as decorative.

## Why operative

Brand canon making any claim in customer-arc, rites, or threshold
territory inherits this fault line. A brand whose customer-arc
canon treats journey as metaphor will produce onboarding that
*describes* a threshold without *staging* one. A brand whose
customer-arc treats passage as territorial will architect actual
liminal moments — moments where the customer is held in a defined
neutral zone with the rules of ordinary life suspended, and
incorporated through actual changed-state.

The marketing-side compression is operationally cheaper (write the
copy, ship the page). The van Gennep discipline is operationally
expensive (architect the actual passage). The cheap version
underperforms in retention and evangelism because the customer
never *crossed* anything.

## Pressure-test signature

When canon claims a customer-arc beat — threshold-crossing,
liminal-period, incorporation — check: is the brand staging an
actual passage with territorial substrate (a defined boundary, a
suspended-ordinary-rules period, an incorporation rite that
reintegrates the customer in changed state)? Or is the canon naming
the beat as a copy gesture without territorial substrate?

A canon claim that uses passage-vocabulary without territorial
substrate is the metaphor failure. The check is whether you can
point at the threshold, the liminal zone, and the incorporation rite
as concrete brand structures — not as page sections.

## Resolution direction

Van Gennep wins on rites and customer-arc. The territorial substrate
is what makes the schema operative; without it the schema collapses
into the marketing-side compression. This is the lineage-repair
the engine exists to perform: recover what Miller and the journey-
compressions had to drop to fit a homepage.

For workshop satellites: any canon write that names a threshold
should be testable against "where is the territorial substrate?"
If the answer is "it's the metaphor", the canon write is failing
at the substrate level.

This tension is operative for [[mythos-customer-arc]],
[[mythos-rites]], and any anti-pattern work on
metaphorical-passage-without-territorial-anchor.

## Contributions

### From [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage|The Rites of Passage]]

The thesis is stated at [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p377]]:
"These phrases and events are seldom meant as 'symbols'; for the
semicivilized the passage is actually a territorial passage. In
fact, the spatial separation of distinct groups is an aspect of
social organization." The whole of Ch II derives the schema from
the threshold made of stone before any social application is
attempted. Van Gennep's order of derivation is itself the rebuke
of the metaphor reading. The marketing-side counterparties
(Miller, Vogler) have not been source-read; their compression
will be sharpened against this tension when their reads land.

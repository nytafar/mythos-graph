# Desacralization

## Operational definition

The historical-existential process by which the cosmos becomes
"opaque, inert, mute" — the long withdrawal of the sacred from
the experience of the world that produces the modern condition of
homo profanus. Distinct from temporary profanation (a sacred site
defiled, a rite broken) and from secularisation in the political-
institutional sense (the separation of religion from state).
Desacralization is the slow loss of the sacred *mode of being in
the world*, the loss of the cipher-quality of the cosmos itself.

Operative consequence: the corpus reads desacralization as the
diagnosis Eliade names against the modern condition. Modern man
is "the descendant of homo religiosus" (per
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p270]]) — formed by opposition to
the religious mode his ancestors lived in, "haunted by the
realities that he has refused and denied"
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p271]]. Desacralization does not
abolish the structures the religious mode produced; it strips
their content. The structures persist as residue, as crypto-
religious behavior, as camouflaged sacred — see
[[camouflaged-sacred]].

## Contributions

### From [[eliade-sacred-and-profane]]

Eliade names desacralization in the introduction to *The Sacred
and the Profane* and develops it across the whole book; chapter
IV closes on it. The first operative claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p011]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p012]]:
"the completely profane world, the wholly desacralized cosmos, is
a recent discovery in the history of the human spirit… modern man
has desacralized his world and assumed a profane existence…
desacralization pervades the entire experience of the nonreligious
man of modern societies."

The crypto-religious-residue claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p022]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p024]]:
the secular man's "privileged places" — birthplace, scenes of
first love — "still retain an exceptional, a unique quality;
they are the 'holy places' of his private universe." Eliade names
this "crypto-religious behavior." The discipline: desacralization
in pure state is a limit, not an achievement. The modern person
preserves the structure of religious experience even when the
content has been disclaimed.

The paradigmatic-images-survive-in-language claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p061]]: "the paradigmatic images
live on in the language and clichés of nonreligious man.
Something of the religious conception of the world still persists
in the behavior of profane man, although he is not always
conscious of this immemorial heritage." The Western language of
"chaos," "disorder," "darkness threatening the world" is
desacralized cosmology kept as cliché. The cosmology produced the
images; desacralization stripped the images of their referent;
the images remained.

The pessimistic-cycle claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p152]]: "But repetition emptied of
its religious content necessarily leads to a pessimistic vision of
existence. When it is no longer a vehicle for reintegrating a
primordial situation, and hence for recovering the mysterious
presence of the gods, that is, when it is desacralized, time
becomes terrifying; it is seen as a circle forever turning on
itself, repeating itself to infinity." The same structural cycle
that was livable inside the religious frame becomes terror outside
it. See [[eternal-return]].

The terrifying-modern-time claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p157]]: "Definitively desacralized,
time presents itself as a precarious and evanescent duration,
leading irremediably to death."

The deus-otiosus connection at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p177]]: the high god abandoned by
fertility-cult specialists is one form of partial desacralization
— the celestial sacred withdrawing while the earth-bound sacred
remains. "In discovering the sacredness of life, man let himself
be increasingly carried away by his own discovery; he gave himself
up to vital hierophanies and turned from the sacrality that
transcended his immediate and daily needs."

The China-miniature-gardens case at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p210]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p217]]
is the operative case-study Eliade uses to show desacralization as
*incremental* rather than total. The Chinese miniature garden
(stones in a perfumed-water bowl, with dwarf trees, miniature
houses, pagodas, bridges) had a long religious history: cosmos in
miniature, Isles of the Blessed, Taoist Immortals. By the
seventeenth century these were "art objects" valued by literati;
the religious meaning had attenuated. But Eliade's claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p217]]: "in the Far East what is
called the 'esthetic emotion' still retains a religious
dimension, even among intellectuals." Desacralization is a
direction, not a state. The garden moves from cosmic ritual
toward "art for art's sake" without ever becoming pure profane
object.

The Christian-author preservation at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p222]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p223]]:
Léon Bloy's "Whether life is in men, in animals, or in plants, it
is Life, and when the minute, the inapprehensible point that is
called death comes, it is always Jesus who departs, alike from a
tree and from a human being." The Christian author preserves the
cosmic-life intuition that institutional Christianity in
industrial societies has largely abandoned (see
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p244]]).

The closing diagnosis at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p243]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p244]]:
"for the nonreligious men of the modern age, the cosmos has
become opaque, inert, mute; it transmits no message, it holds no
cipher." Urban Christianity has lost "the cosmic values that it
still possessed in the Middle Ages"; "in these man-God-history
relationships there is no place for the cosmos. From this it
would appear that, even for a genuine Christian, the world is no
longer felt as the work of God."

The closing chapter of the book at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p269]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p281]]
gives the most-developed treatment. Modern nonreligious man "is
the result of a process of desacralization. Just as nature is the
product of a progressive secularization of the cosmos as the
work of God, profane man is the result of a desacralization of
human existence"
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p270]]. The "second fall" diagnosis
at [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p280]]: from the Christian point
of view, "nonreligion is equivalent to a new 'fall' of man — in
other words… nonreligious man has lost the capacity to live
religion consciously, and hence to understand and assume it; but…
in his deepest being, he still retains a memory of it… After the
first 'fall,' the religious sense descended to the level of the
'divided' consciousness; now, after the second, it has fallen
even further, into the depths of the unconscious; it has been
forgotten."

For the corpus: desacralization is the operative diagnosis of the
modern condition that the engine works against. The engine's
mission, stated in the north-star, is reverse-compression toward
the source the marketing-side compressions abandoned. Eliade gives
the concept that names what has been lost. Tightly paired with
[[camouflaged-sacred]] — the desacralization produces the
camouflaged-sacred residue, which produces the camouflaged-sacred
anti-pattern (see [[camouflaged-sacred-in-modernity]]).

The Norwegian substrate's relation to desacralization is mixed.
The Lutheran reform desacralized parts of the Catholic-medieval
substrate; the post-1960s secularisation desacralized parts of
the Lutheran substrate; pre-Christian substrate elements survive
in folkway, place-name, seasonal practice. Wave 3 will populate
the substrate-specific reading.

## Norwegian substrate

### Confirmations

- The substrate confirms desacralization as historical-existential
  process. The 19th-20th-c. Norwegian secularisation moves
  through the same shape Eliade names at
  [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p270]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p281]]:
  cosmological-religious mode of inhabitation thinning, structures
  persisting as residue, the cosmos becoming "opaque."
- The Lutheran rupture (early 16th c.) confirms a partial
  desacralization Eliade can describe — the displacement of the
  Catholic-medieval sacramental cosmology by an interiorised
  Word-and-faith register. The substrate carries the precedent
  at sufficient density to enter Eliade's diagnostic frame.

### Augmentations

- The substrate augments via **the Lutheran rupture's specific
  shape**. The Norwegian-substrate desacralization is *not*
  the modern Western default Eliade diagnoses generically — it
  has a specific double-rupture history. The Lutheran reform
  *flattened sacred-space* (no transubstantiation of place; the
  sacred-everywhere-and-nowhere of Word-religion replaced the
  located-sacred of saints, relics, holy places). Then the
  19th-20th-c. modernisation compounded the flattening with
  Eliade's "second fall" pattern. The substrate carries
  *layered desacralization* — Lutheran-shape over
  Catholic-medieval ground, modernist over Lutheran ground —
  not a single-step modernisation.
- The substrate augments via **the secular-national-romantic
  filling**. The vacuum left by the Lutheran flattening was
  partly filled by the secular-national-romantic substrate
  (Bjørnson, Wergeland, Asbjørnsen-Moe-Lie, Welhaven, the
  17. mai civic-religion, the late-19th-c. nation-building).
  This is *partially-religious in form*: the structures of
  sacred-civic worship (the parade, the flag-raising, the
  national-romantic mountain-and-fjord aesthetic) carrying
  religious shape without Lutheran-confessional content. The
  substrate has been *occupying-the-vacuum* for over a century;
  this is the camouflaged-sacred at civic-substrate scale.
- The substrate augments via the **survival of pre-Christian
  substrate elements** (place-names, folkway, seasonal practice)
  through the Christian-Lutheran layer and out the other side
  into modernity. The substrate carries multi-layer
  desacralization with each layer leaving residue at the next.

# Eternal return

## Operational definition

The doctrine that sacred time is reversible and recoverable: the
festival, the rite, the seasonal renewal makes the founding instant
present *again*, and again, indefinitely. The world is not running
forward toward novelty; it is renewed by periodic return to the
illud tempus. The eternal return is the operative shape of sacred
time experienced as cyclic — and the term Eliade uses to describe
the structure that holds across primitive, archaic, Indian, and
Greek thought.

Operative consequence: the eternal return is not a metaphysical
optimism about cycles; it is a structural feature of religious
time. *Without* its religious meaning, the same cyclic structure
becomes terrifying — a circle "forever turning on itself, repeating
itself to infinity." The eternal return holds together as
operative only inside the cosmology that gives the return its
content; strip the cosmology and you keep the cycle and lose the
return.

## Contributions

### From [[eliade-sacred-and-profane]]

Eliade names the eternal return as the operative structure of
sacred time. The reversibility claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p085]]: sacred time "always remains
equal to itself, it neither changes nor is exhausted. With each
periodical festival, the participants find the same sacred time
the same that had been manifested in the festival of the
previous year or in the festival of a century earlier." The same
sacred time is met again and again — not the next iteration of a
similar festival but the same instant.

The succession-of-eternities formula at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p119]]: sacred time is "always the
same, that it is 'a succession of eternities.'" Each festival is
its own eternity; the calendar is the succession of these
eternities, each indefinitely recoverable. The participant
"emerges from their historical time… and recovers primordial time,
which is always the same, which belongs to eternity."

The nostalgia-for-return claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p126]]: "religious man — especially
the religious man of primitive societies — is above all a man
paralyzed by the myth of the eternal return." Eliade writes this
in modern-psychologist register, anticipating the diagnosis:
return-discipline can look from outside like fixation, like
refusal of the new, like nostalgia in the pejorative sense. The
two-page argument that follows
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p127]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p129]]
defends the eternal return as a positive religious orientation —
"thirst for the sacred and nostalgia for being" — not as
psychological evasion.

The sacred-calendar-as-eternal-return claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p149]]: "The sacred calendar
periodically regenerates time, because it makes it coincide with
the time of origin, the strong, pure time."

The Indian-philosophy case at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p151]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p154]]
is the operative warning. Eliade traces the doctrine of cosmic
cycles — the *mahāyuga* of 12,000 divine years (4,320,000 human
years), the *kalpa* as a day in Brahma's life, the indefinite
succession of cosmic creations and destructions. "This is the true
eternal return, the eternal repetition of the fundamental rhythm
of the cosmos — its periodical destruction and recreation. In
short, it is the conception of the Year-Cosmos, but emptied of its
religious content"
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p154]]. The Indian elites who held
the doctrine in its philosophical form found it terrifying — the
same return, but without the participation in being that made the
primitive return livable. "Time was homologized to the cosmic
illusion, and the eternal return to existence signified indefinite
prolongation of suffering and slavery." The hope was nonreturn —
*nirvana* as escape from the cycle.

The Greek extension at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p154]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p155]]
through Puech: Pythagoreans, Stoics, Platonists "reached the
point of admitting that within each of these cycles of duration…
the same situations are reproduced that have already been
produced in previous cycles and will be reproduced in subsequent
cycles." Cosmic duration as repetition and *anakuklōsis*. The
eternal return as terror.

The Judaeo-Christian break at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p155]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p156]]:
"Judaism presents an innovation of the first importance. For
Judaism, time has a beginning and will have an end. The idea of
cyclic time is left behind." Christianity extends this through
the historicity of Christ — sacred time becomes historical-but-
sanctified rather than cyclic-mythical. The break with the eternal
return is decisive: "history reveals itself to be a new dimension
of the presence of God in the world."

The modern condition at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p157]]: "Definitively desacralized,
time presents itself as a precarious and evanescent duration,
leading irremediably to death." Modern philosophical historicism
inherits the linear time of Christianity but disclaims the
sanctification — and the result is "no longer conceived as a
circle, time in these modern philosophies once again wears the
terrifying aspect that it wore in the Indian and Greek philosophies
of the eternal return." The cycle is gone, but the terror is back.

For the corpus: eternal return is the structural concept that
makes sacred-calendar work as more than schedule. The calendar
returns; the return is to the same illud tempus; the return is what
regenerates time. The Norwegian substrate carries a much sharper
break with Eliade's universal claim than Christianity does:
[[ragnarok]] is the irreversible end. Norse cosmology has a
linear-final shape that the cyclic eternal return does not
describe. This is the sharpest single-point tension between
Eliade and the Norwegian substrate, and it fires on every dimension
that uses sacred-calendar work. Wave 4 will write the explicit
tension node; flag here as `eliade-vs-norse-cyclical-recovery-vs-
non-final-death`.

### Breaks

- **Ragnarǫk breaks Eliade's eternal-return at the cosmological
  level.** Eliade's claim at
  [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p085]] is that sacred time is
  "indefinitely recoverable, indefinitely repeatable." Norse
  cosmology has Ragnarǫk as **irreversible end**: the wolf
  swallows the sun, Yggdrasil falls, the gods die in their
  appointed combats, the world is consumed in fire. The
  post-Ragnarǫk Gimli is *not* the same world recovered through
  reactualisation; it is a new world after the irreversible
  end of the old one. Norse cosmological time is *linear-final*,
  not *cyclic-recoverable*.
- Norse non-final death (the einherjar at Valhǫll fight, die,
  feast, fight again across the days leading to Ragnarǫk) is
  *not eternal-return* in Eliade's sense — it is *episodic
  recurrence inside a temporally-bounded preparation* for the
  irreversible end. See existing tension flag at
  [[van-gennep-symbolic-rebirth-vs-norse-non-final-death]].
- Flag for wave 4 as `eliade-vs-norse-cyclical-recovery-vs-non-
  final-death`. This is the sharpest cosmological break against
  Eliade after Yggdrasil-as-gnawed-axis. Both fire on every
  dimension that uses sacred-calendar work; both pressure the
  brand-mythos default of *return-to-origin* by adding
  *anticipated end* as cosmological weight.

### Augmentations

- The substrate augments via **eschatologically-anchored
  sacred-calendar**. The Norwegian sacred-calendar carries
  return-discipline (jul, påske, jonsok, olsok all reactualise
  founding instants) but the cosmological frame the calendar
  sits in is *forward-pointing*. Brands inheriting the
  substrate carry both modes: return-to-founding *and*
  preparation-for-end. This is augmentation against an
  Eliadean frame that runs only the recovery-mode.
- The substrate augments via **the gullaldr / Gimli pairing**.
  The Norse golden age (gullaldr) is paradisal-past, but Gimli
  is paradisal-future. Both modes operate. See
  [[nostalgia-for-paradise]] substrate section for the parallel.

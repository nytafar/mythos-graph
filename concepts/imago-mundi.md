# Imago mundi

## Operational definition

The image of the world reproduced at smaller scale. A village, a
city, a temple, a house, a sacred lodge, a body — each can be an
imago mundi: a microcosmic replica of the cosmic structure, with
its own centre, its own four directions, its own break-in-plane
upward, its own foundation downward. The replica is not symbolic
shorthand for the cosmos; it *is* a cosmos, at the scale of the
inhabited unit.

Operative consequence: religious habitation is always cosmological.
To build a house is to build a small cosmos; to settle a territory
is to consecrate it as an imago mundi; to lay out a temple is to
inscribe the world in stone. The brand that situates its workspace,
its retail space, its product as imago mundi is making a
cosmological claim — the structure of the brand's cosmos is
visible at the scale of the inhabited object.

## Contributions

### From [[eliade-sacred-and-profane]]

Eliade gives the formula at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p052]]: "An entire country, a city,
a sanctuary, all equally well present an imago mundi." The
context is Flavius Josephus on the Temple — "the court represented
the sea, the Holy Place represented earth, and the Holy of Holies
heaven" — and the operative claim: "both the imago mundi and the
Center are repeated in the inhabited world. Palestine, Jerusalem,
and the Temple severally and concurrently represent the image of
the universe and the Center of the World."

The proliferation-of-centres claim immediately following at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p053]]: "this multiplication of
centers and this reiteration of the image of the world on smaller
and smaller scales constitute one of the specific characteristics
of traditional societies." The country contains the city, the city
contains the temple, the temple contains the holy of holies — and
each level is its own imago mundi. The Achilpa carry the sacred
pole because they need always to be at the centre of *their*
imago mundi, whatever its current location.

The village case at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p055]]: "in Bali, as in some parts of
Asia, when a new village is to be built the people look for a
natural intersection, where two roads cross at right angles. A
square constructed from a central point is an imago mundi." The
village is structured as cosmic image: division into four sections
"corresponds to the division of the universe into four horizons"; a
ceremonial house at the centre with its roof "symbolically
representing heaven"; the world of the dead at the perpendicular
opposite. The village is not metaphorically cosmic; it is
cosmically structured.

The habitation case at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p064]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p068]]:
"the habitation always undergoes a process of sanctification,
because it constitutes an imago mundi and the world is a divine
creation." Two methods: assimilation by horizon-projection or
axis-mundi installation; or repetition of the cosmogonic
slaying-and-fashioning. Either way, "the house is an imago mundi.
The sky is conceived as a vast tent supported by a central pillar;
the tent pole or the central post of the house is assimilated to
the Pillars of the World."

The temple-as-imago-mundi case at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p077]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p078]]:
the Babylonian sanctuary, the Jerusalem Temple, the Christian
basilica and Byzantine church each function as imago mundi — the
sacred edifice "incarnates and at the same time sanctifies the
world." The Byzantine church passage at p078 spells out the
microcosmic mapping: four parts symbolising four cardinal
directions, altar as paradise, west as realm of darkness and
death, middle as earth, dome as cosmic vault.

The sacred lodge as imago mundi at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p092]]: among the Algonquins and the
Sioux, "the sacred lodge represents the universe; but at the same
time it symbolizes the year." The four doors and four windows
correspond to the four cardinal directions; "the Year is a circle
around the world" — that is, around the sacred lodge. The imago
mundi carries both spatial and temporal cosmic structure.

The body extension at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p237]]: "His dwelling is a microcosm;
and so too is his body. The homology house-body-cosmos presents
itself very early." The full body-house-cosmos development at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p237]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p243]]
extends the imago mundi into the human body itself — the spinal
column as cosmic pillar, the navel/heart as Centre of the World,
the brahmarandhra as upper opening. This is treated as its own
operative concept; see [[body-house-cosmos]].

For the corpus: imago mundi is the structural concept that
connects [[axis-mundi]] (the central image's vertical figure),
[[center-of-the-world]] (the located point), and
[[body-house-cosmos]] (the homology that runs the imago mundi
through every scale of inhabitation). When a brand asks "what is
the structure of our space?" it is asking an imago mundi
question. The answer is operative when each scale (workspace,
retail, product, communication) reproduces the same structure.
The Norwegian substrate's gard structure (with its inner-outer
gradients, central tun, peripheral utmark) is imago-mundi-shaped
in substrate-native form; wave 3 fills.

## Norwegian substrate

### Confirmations

- The substrate carries imago mundi at three nested scales — gard
  (the farmstead as cosmos: tun centre, innmark as ordered, utmark
  as wild-edge, the surrounding fence as horizon-equivalent);
  langhus (the long-house as imago mundi at habitation scale,
  with high-seat / hearth / smoke-hole each carrying cosmological
  function); stove (the room with hjørnegull, ildsted, takbjelke
  carrying the small-scale repetition). The proliferation Eliade
  names at [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p053]] is operative across
  all three.
- The gardstre confirms the world-axis-in-habitation pattern at
  [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p068]] — the central tree of the
  gard is the substrate's instance of central-pillar-as-axis-mundi.

### Augmentations

- The substrate augments via **the gard's hierarchical-cosmological
  layout**. Tunet (centre) → innmark (cultivated near-zone) →
  utmark (wild far-zone) → utgard (cosmologically-charged
  beyond-zone) is a four-zone schema with operative ordering
  Eliade's "country / city / sanctuary" proliferation does not
  capture. The gard is not just an imago mundi at one scale; it
  is an imago mundi *with internal cosmological gradient*.
- The substrate augments via **the langhus-as-cosmos**: the long
  hall's high-seat (høgsete) carries sovereignty function; the
  hearth (langild) carries the sacred-fire centre; the smoke-hole
  (ljore / røykhul) carries the upper-opening / brahmarandhra
  function (cf. [[body-house-cosmos]]). This is imago-mundi at
  habitation scale with the four-part schema fully populated by
  substrate-native names.
- The substrate augments via **the legal-customary expression** —
  åseterett (the eldest's right to inherit the gard intact), odel
  (the inheritance-bond), the gard-as-legal-person all extend the
  imago mundi into a *legal-cosmological* register Eliade does
  not reach. The gard is not just structured-as-cosmos; it is
  *named in law as cosmos-equivalent*.

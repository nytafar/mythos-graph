# Sacred time

## Operational definition

Time as religious man experiences it: nonhomogeneous, structured by
sacred intervals (festivals, rites, liturgical periods) and profane
duration (ordinary days). Sacred time is reversible — it can be
re-entered through ritual — whereas profane time runs irreversibly
from event to event. The chapter-level category of which
[[illud-tempus]] (the founding instant), [[eternal-return]] (the
cyclic structure of return), and [[paradigmatic-model]] (the myth
that founds the rite) are operative terms.

Operative consequence: a sacred-calendar is not a list of
observances. It is the temporal armature of a cosmology — the
grid of sacred intervals through which the founding instant is
periodically made present again. Without the calendar, the illud
tempus is unreachable; without the illud tempus, the calendar is
empty schedule.

## Contributions

### From [[eliade-sacred-and-profane]]

Sacred time is the subject of *The Sacred and the Profane* chapter
II [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p084]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p157]].
The chapter opens with the operative pair to chapter I:

> For religious man time too, like space, is neither homogeneous
> nor continuous. On the one hand there are the intervals of a
> sacred time, the time of festivals; on the other there is
> profane time, ordinary temporal duration… by means of rite
> religious man can pass without danger from ordinary temporal
> duration to sacred time.
> [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p084]]

The break-in-homogeneity structure that defines [[sacred-space]]
applies symmetrically to sacred time. The two concepts are
inseparable in Eliade's frame.

The reversibility claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p085]]: sacred time is "indefinitely
recoverable, indefinitely repeatable." It is "an ontological,
Parmenidean time; it always remains equal to itself, it neither
changes nor is exhausted." This is the structural difference from
profane time, which runs irreversibly toward death. The festival
re-enters the same time; the ordinary day proceeds.

The chapter develops sacred time across registers: the
templum/tempus etymological pairing
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p091]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p094]]
(cosmos and year homologised; the temple sanctifies cosmic life;
the year is built each time the altar is built); the annual
repetition of creation
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p096]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p100]]
(akitu, Enuma elish, Nawroz); the regeneration-through-return
section [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p101]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p111]];
festival time and structure
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p112]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p123]];
contemporaneity with the gods
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p124]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p130]];
myth as paradigmatic model
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p131]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p148]];
sacred history vs. historicism
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p149]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p157]].

The chapter's central operative claim, stated in many forms:
sacred time is the time of the gods' creative acts; the festival
re-enters that time; the participant becomes contemporary with
the gods by ritual reactualisation of what the gods did *in illo
tempore*. The sacred-calendar is "the periodical reactualization
of the creative acts performed by the divine beings"
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p112]].

The cosmos-and-year homology at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p091]]: "in a number of North
American Indian languages the term *world* (=Cosmos) is also used
in the sense of *year*. The Yokuts say 'the world has passed,'
meaning 'a year has gone by.'" The cosmos and the year are the
same divine work read at different scales; sacred time is what
gives the year its cosmological weight.

The desacralized version at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p157]]: "Definitively desacralized,
time presents itself as a precarious and evanescent duration,
leading irremediably to death." The modern condition strips sacred
time and leaves only profane duration — and profane duration in
its pure form "wears the terrifying aspect that it wore in the
Indian and Greek philosophies of the eternal return." This is the
closing diagnosis: when the religious meaning is removed, cyclic
time becomes terrifying because there is nothing to return *to*.

For the corpus: sacred time is operative for the sacred-calendar
dimension and for the customer-arc dimension. The customer-arc
read against sacred time becomes a structured calendar of
encounters with the brand's founding instant — campaigns as
festivals, milestones as liturgical markers, the customer's first
contact and recurring contacts read as ritual returns rather than
funnel touchpoints. The Norwegian substrate's calendar density
populates sacred time at substrate-native resolution; wave 3.

## Norwegian substrate

### Confirmations

- The Norwegian calendar (jul, fastelavn, påske, 17. mai,
  jonsok / midtsommer, olsok, høsttakkefest, slaktetiden,
  innhaustinga) confirms sacred-time at substrate scale — each
  carries the festival-as-sacred-interval discipline Eliade
  names at [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p084]].
- [[seterliv]] confirms the seasonal sanctified-time pattern:
  the seter-summer is a qualitatively-different temporal zone,
  marked by departure (seterferd) and return (heimferd), with
  its own customary practices. The seter-summer is not just a
  *time of year*; it is a *kind of time*.

### Breaks

(none surfaced in this read at structural level — the break in
sacred-time / cyclic-recovery lives in [[eternal-return]] where
Ragnarǫk's irreversibility pressures Eliade's Parmenidean-
recoverable claim)

### Augmentations

- The substrate augments sacred-time via [[dugnad]] —
  **sanctified communal labour-time**. Eliade's sacred-time is
  primarily *receptive* (the festival re-enters the founding
  instant); dugnad runs sacred-time as *productive shared
  labour*. The work itself sanctifies the interval; the closing
  meal incorporates participants into a bond that persists past
  the rite. This is sacred-time-as-shared-work, an extension into
  a register Eliade's frame does not articulate.
- The substrate augments via **rank-suspension during sacred-
  time**. Russefeiring as the sharpest case (the russedress
  flattens hierarchies; the russetid suspends ordinary social
  rules across all participants); 17. mai as the civic case (the
  korps marches, the bygd parades, social rank is operatively
  suspended for the day); dugnad as the labour case. This is
  the *liminal-as-equaliser* property the vG read surfaced —
  here augmenting Eliade's frame in addition to vG's.
- The substrate augments via **ærsetikk-anchored sacred-time**:
  the binding of *ord* (word as oath) creates time-zones inside
  the social fabric. A given word generates a time-promise the
  ætt is obligated to honor across time. This is honour-ethics
  carrying sacred-time discipline at social-fabric scale, not
  at festival scale.

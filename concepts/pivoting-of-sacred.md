# Pivoting of the sacred

## Operational definition

The doctrine that sacredness is not absolute but relational. A position
that is profane in one frame is sacred in another, and the same person
moves through alternating sacred and profane states across a lifetime.
The sacred-profane axis pivots around the subject and around the
situation; it is not fixed to particular objects, places, or persons.

Operative consequence: the rite of passage is the mechanism by which
the pivot is enacted safely. Without rites, the change of state would
expose the social body to the dangers of unmediated contact between
incompatible sacred fields.

## Contributions

### From [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage]]

van Gennep states the doctrine at
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p030]]: "Sacredness as an attribute is
not absolute; it is brought into play by the nature of particular
situations." The example sequence: "A man at home, in his tribe, lives
in the secular realm; he moves into the realm of the sacred when he
goes on a journey and finds himself a foreigner near a camp of
strangers… every woman, though congenitally impure, is sacred to all
adult men; if she is pregnant, she also becomes sacred to all other
women of the tribe except her close relatives."

The territorial extension at [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p039]]:
"the territories on either side of the neutral zone are sacred in
relation to whoever is in the zone, but the zone, in turn, is sacred
for the inhabitants of the adjacent territories." Sacredness is
indexical to position.

The doctrine is what allows the schema to absorb apparent contradictions:
the stranger is sacred (relative to the village) and weak (relative to
his own world he has left); the pregnant woman is sacred (to other women)
and impure (in the men's frame); the novice is dead (to childhood) and
not-yet-born (to adulthood). The "magic circles pivot, shifting as a
person moves from one place in society to another"
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p030]].

This is van Gennep's most explicit anti-essentialist move. Frazer and
Smith treated "the sacred" as a property of objects; van Gennep treats
it as a structural relation. The corpus reads this against
[[eliade]]'s later essentialist recovery — Eliade's hierophany asserts
that some places, times, and objects *are* sacred, not merely sacred-
in-relation. Pressure-test territory.

## Norwegian substrate

### Confirmations
- [[allemannsretten]] confirms pivoting-of-sacred at the legal-
  customary level: the wanderer is in-but-not-of the territory, with
  the 150 m threshold rule encoding precisely the magic-circle pivot
  van Gennep names at [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p030]]. The
  wanderer's status pivots as they cross from utmark to innmark.
- The Norwegian substrate's customary distinction between *gjest* and
  *fremmed* confirms the pivot: the same person occupies both status
  positions across the threshold of invitation. Their sacredness (in
  the relational sense) is indexical to position.

### Breaks
- The substrate's [[gard-utgard]] structure carries a partial break
  against pure pivoting. The boundary between gard (settled, human,
  ordered) and utgard (wild, troldsk, governed by hulder, jutul,
  draug) is *topographically fixed* in a way van Gennep's pivoting-as-
  relation underdescribes. The substrate sacred has both relational
  (the wanderer pivots) and locational (the woods are utgard
  regardless of who walks them) properties. van Gennep's frame
  predicts only the relational layer.

### Augmentations
- *Pivoting-of-the-everyday* in substrate practice. Norwegian
  domestic sacred (jul, midtsommer) operates by pivoting ordinary
  household time into sacred time — the same kjøkken is profane
  on tuesday and sacred on lille julaften. van Gennep's frame
  predicts the pivot; the substrate's calendar-density (jul,
  fastelavn, påske, 17. mai, midtsommer/jonsok, olsok, høst-rytme)
  populates the pivoting at higher resolution than van Gennep's
  examples. Pressure-test territory against [[eliade]]'s
  illud-tempus when Eliade is source-read.

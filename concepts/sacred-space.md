# Sacred space

## Operational definition

Space as religious man experiences it: nonhomogeneous, structured by
breaks and qualitative differences, with sacred zones and profane
expanses arranged around fixed points. The sacred-space concept is
the chapter-level category of which [[hierophany]],
[[axis-mundi]], [[center-of-the-world]], and [[imago-mundi]] are
specific operative terms. Sacred space is the field; the others
are the structures inside it.

Operative consequence: a religious cosmology is, first, a sacred-
space cosmology. The brand that has not located its sacred zones
(what is held inviolable, what is set apart, what is the centre)
has not yet articulated its cosmology. Sacred space is what makes
orientation possible — and orientation, in Eliade's frame, is what
makes any world possible at all.

## Contributions

### From [[eliade-sacred-and-profane]]

Sacred space is the subject of *The Sacred and the Profane*
chapter I [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p018]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p082]].
The chapter opens with the operative claim:

> For religious man, space is not homogeneous; he experiences
> interruptions, breaks in it; some parts of space are
> qualitatively different from others… the religious experience of
> the nonhomogeneity of space is a primordial experience,
> homologizable to a founding of the world.
> [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p018]]

Sacred space is not a region designated by religious authority; it
is a structural feature of how religious man experiences space at
all. Eliade contrasts this throughout the chapter with profane
space's "homogeneity and… relativity"
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p022]] — the geometric space of the
desacralized condition, where "no qualitative differentiation
and, hence, no orientation are given by virtue of its inherent
structure."

The break-in-plane definition at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p019]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p020]]:
"For it is the break effected in space that allows the world to
be constituted, because it reveals the fixed point, the central
axis for all future orientation." Sacred space *is* this break.
Where the sacred manifests, space becomes structured; where space
becomes structured, a world becomes possible.

The Eliade chapter develops sacred space across four registers:
the temple/sanctuary case
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p025]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p030]],
the cosmogonic case (consecration repeats creation)
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p036]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p043]],
the system-of-the-world case (axis mundi, center, imago mundi)
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p044]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p057]],
and the habitation case (the house as imago mundi)
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p062]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p078]].

Each register is one expression of the same structure: sacred
space is bounded, oriented, founded by hierophany or by ritual
imitation of cosmogony, and connected to the cosmic regions
through an axis. The chapter's full closing claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p080]]: "the experience of sacred
space makes possible the 'founding of the world': where the sacred
manifests itself in space, the real unveils itself, the world
comes into existence." Sacred space is what makes any cosmos
cosmos at all.

The ontological-thirst claim at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p081]] reads sacred space as the
correlate of religious man's "will to take his stand at the very
heart of the real, at the Center of the World." The desire to
inhabit sacred space is the desire to be where being is most
concentrated. This grounds [[center-of-the-world]] and
[[imago-mundi]] in a deeper drive than merely cosmological
preference.

The relation to [[sacred-time]] at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p083]]: "The experience of sacred
time will make it possible for religious man periodically to
experience the cosmos as it was *in principio*." The two concepts
are paired: sacred space organises the where; sacred time
organises the when. They share the same break-in-homogeneity
structure. The reader of *The Sacred and the Profane* meets them
as a pair across chapters I and II.

For the corpus: sacred space is the chapter-level category that
holds the family of cosmological concepts together. When a brand
locates its retreat, its sanctuary, its centre, its threshold, it
is doing sacred-space work. The Norwegian substrate's seter,
gravhaug, kirke, and the broader gard-utgard structure populate
sacred space at substrate-native scale; wave 3 fills.

## Norwegian substrate

### Confirmations

- [[gard-utgard]] confirms sacred-space as nonhomogeneous
  qualitative-differentiation at substrate scale.
- [[seterliv]] confirms sacred-space at the seter — the summer
  pasture is a qualitatively-different zone with its own customary
  rules, its own sacred markers, its own break-in-homogeneity
  from the home gard.
- The gravhaug, the hellige steiner, the bygdeborg, Stiklestad,
  Borgund stavkirke each confirm Eliade's claim at
  [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p018]] that sacred-space is not
  a designation but a structural feature of how religious-mode
  inhabitants experience the landscape.

### Breaks

(none surfaced in this read — sacred-space holds at substrate
scale; the substrate-specific cosmology pattern is augmentation,
not break)

### Augmentations

- The substrate augments sacred-space with
  [[allemannsretten]] — the operative claim that
  **sacred-space-as-shared-resource** is a substrate principle
  Eliade's individual-encounter framing underdescribes. The
  landscape carries hierophanic charge; access to that
  hierophanic charge is *legally and customarily distributed*
  rather than priestly-mediated. The Norwegian substrate has no
  temple-cult monopoly on sacred-space; the customary access-
  pattern *is* the cosmology's operative claim about
  sacred-space's mode of belonging.
- The 150 m threshold in friluftsloven is the codified-modern
  instance: sacred-space-as-shared, with the proximity-to-
  habitation rule carrying the operative discipline of where
  the shared sacred yields to private sacred.
- The substrate augments via the **distributed multi-scale
  pattern**: sacred-space at gard scale (the tun), at seter
  scale (the summer-pasture), at landscape scale (the
  gravhaug-network across a bygd), at national scale (Stiklestad,
  the kirke, Akershus). Eliade's frame predicts proliferation
  of centres at
  [[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p053]]; the substrate runs the
  proliferation with each scale governed by its own customary
  access-discipline.

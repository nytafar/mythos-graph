# Territorial passage

## Operational definition

The crossing of a physical boundary between two territories — frontier,
threshold, portal, river, mountain pass, doorway. In van Gennep's frame,
territorial passage is not one application of rites of passage among
many; it is the paradigmatic case from which the schema is read off.
Social passage of every kind is identified with — not merely metaphorised
by — territorial passage.

The practical claim: when a passage is enacted, it is enacted in space
first. The movement of the body across a real boundary is the rite. Other
forms of passage (age-group transitions, marriage, ordination, adoption)
borrow the structure from the territorial case, often by reproducing it
literally inside the ceremony (the bride crossing the threshold, the
novice entering the sacred enclosure).

## Contributions

### From [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage]]

van Gennep names territorial passage as the framework at
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p033]] and develops it across Ch II as
the chapter from which all other chapters proceed. The neutral zone
between territories at [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p039]] gives the
liminal its concrete instantiation: the traveller "wavers between two
worlds" in actual desert, marsh, or virgin forest before he wavers
between two states.

The portal-rites at [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p042]],
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p043]] establish the threshold as a
sacred zone in itself: "the door is the boundary between the foreign
and domestic worlds in the case of an ordinary dwelling, between the
profane and sacred worlds in the case of a temple. Therefore to cross
the threshold is to unite oneself with a new world."

van Gennep's most explicit theoretical claim about territorial passage
appears in the conclusions [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p377]]: "the
passage from one social position to another is identified with a
*territorial passage*… These phrases and events are seldom meant as
'symbols'; for the semicivilized the passage is actually a territorial
passage."

This is the operative anti-metaphor move. The passage is not "like" a
crossing; it *is* a crossing. The brand application surfaces immediately:
a "customer journey" that lacks any territorial enactment — no real
threshold, no actual door, no spatial discontinuity — has the form of a
rite of passage with the substrate removed. The form persists; the
operation does not. See [[metaphorical-passage-without-territorial-anchor]].

## Norwegian substrate

### Confirmations
- [[seterliv]] confirms territorial-passage at the seasonal scale: the
  ferd from home gard to summer mountain pasture is preliminal-
  territorial, the seter itself is liminal sacred-zone (suspended from
  ordinary social order, governed by its own customary rules), and
  the heimferd at autumn is postliminal reincorporation. The substrate
  preserves the territorial-passage structure as actual passage,
  unmetaphorised.
- [[allemannsretten]] confirms van Gennep's territorial paradigm by
  encoding it in customary law: the wanderer-on-foot is granted
  partial-liminal status in the landscape (passage permitted, with
  threshold restrictions — no closer than 150 m to inhabited
  buildings). The wanderer is in the territory but not of it; the
  150 m rule is a codified neutral zone.
- [[gard-utgard]] (the architectural and ethical boundary between the
  farmstead and the wild) confirms threshold and territorial-passage
  as substrate-load-bearing. The boundary is a real fence, with real
  custom about who may cross when and on what terms.

### Breaks
- The Norwegian substrate's [[gard-utgard]] structure is *more
  architecturally rigid* than van Gennep's pivoting-of-sacred metaphor
  predicts. The boundary is fixed in the landscape — not a mobile
  magic circle that pivots around the person, but a stable feature
  the person crosses. van Gennep's pivoting frame (which works for
  the stranger-relation and for the magico-religious dynamism of
  Australian and Polynesian examples) describes a relational sacred;
  the substrate's gard-utgard works as a topographical sacred. Both
  hold; the substrate's territorial fixity restrains the pivoting
  frame's reach.

### Augmentations
- The substrate adds *hospitality-as-rite* (gjestebud) as a more
  developed postliminal-territorial form than van Gennep's reception
  catalogue carries. The Norwegian gjestebud is not just a meal in
  common; it carries customary obligations on host and guest that
  outlast the ceremony — the bond persists for years after, with
  reciprocal expectation. van Gennep's frame can describe the rite;
  the substrate adds the after-life of the bond.

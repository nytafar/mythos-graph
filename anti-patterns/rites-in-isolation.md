# Rites in isolation

## Failure description

Treating ritual elements as isolated curiosities, lifted out of their
ceremonial sequence and compared across cultures by surface
similarity. The phenomenon under study is the *order* of rites within
a ceremony — the position each rite occupies and what that position
does. Extract a rite from its sequence and you have a custom; extract
many rites and compare them by feature, and you have a catalogue of
customs. Neither is a phenomenon. The ceremony's order, the
relational position of each rite, is what the catalogue cannot
recover once it has been broken open.

The failure is upstream of method. Once the analyst's unit of study
has become "the rite" rather than "the order of rites," the
phenomenon they were trying to describe has already disappeared from
their data.

## Source's diagnosis

### From [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage]]

van Gennep diagnoses the failure as the dominant defect of his
disciplinary inheritance — Tylor, Frazer, Crawley, Hartland, the
Mannhardt-Frazer-Smith school. The diagnosis runs explicitly at
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p184]]: "the primary purpose of this
book is precisely to react against the 'folkloristic' or
'anthropological' procedure, which consists of extracting various
rites — whether positive or negative — from a set of ceremonies and
considering them in isolation, thus removing them from a context
which gives them meaning and reveals their position in a dynamic
whole."

The diagnosis is sharpened in the conclusions at
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p373]]: even when scholars noticed
similarities across rites, "all these scholars, including Crawley,
saw only resemblances in particulars." Hartland on the communal meal,
Frazer and Crawley on seclusion and dietary taboos, Diels and
Dieterich on purification rites — the catalogues identified specific
rites recurring across ceremonies. None located the *order* in which
the recurrences occurred, which is what would have rendered them a
phenomenon rather than a list.

van Gennep names the methodological consequence at
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p374]],
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p375]]: "Our interest lies not in the
particular rites but in their essential significance and their
relative positions within ceremonial wholes — that is, their order…
Beneath a multiplicity of forms, either consciously expressed or
merely implied, a typical pattern always recurs: *the pattern of the
rites of passage*."

The applied-compression case at
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p183]] is sharper still: existing
scholarship on the ancient mysteries (Mannhardt, Frazer, Reinach,
Cumont) had treated the rites as sympathetic-fertility magic and
catalogued the elements without ever asking after the order. The
order was the thing the entire mystery cult turned around, and it
was invisible to the inherited method.

## Corrective

The corrective is the unit-of-analysis move. Stop treating any
specific rite as the analytic unit; start treating the sequence as
the unit. A rite is read by its position within an ordered ceremony,
not by comparison with similar rites elsewhere. Comparison across
cultures becomes comparison of *sequences* — preliminal-liminal-
postliminal shape, weighting between phases, recursion of nested
phases, autonomy of intermediate states.

For the corpus, the corrective is operationalised in the per-source
subsection convention: a source's contribution to a concept is read
as a position within that source's overall argument, not as a feature
extracted for comparison. The order of contributions matters.

## Workshop pressure-test relevance

When canon work catalogues elements without grounding them in a
sequence, this anti-pattern fires.

**Pressure-test signature.** A canon document that lists features,
values, principles, or practices without articulating the order
between them — what comes first, what depends on what, what only
makes sense in light of what precedes it. The canon claims
completeness by listing items; the listing is what produces the
collapse.

**What to look for.** Brand documents that read as taxonomies (our
values are: integrity, courage, mastery, joy) without articulating
how the values relate to each other in lived sequence. Customer-arc
documents that name twelve stages without explaining why one stage
must precede another. Mythos work that catalogues archetypes without
locating each archetype's position in the brand's structural
relations.

**Diagnosis voice.** "The list is correct in its parts and
unintelligible as a whole. Each element here is real; the order
between them is missing. Reading this document, the customer cannot
tell whether the brand's claim is sequential or simultaneous —
which means the claim does not yet exist as a claim. Restore the
order; the elements will keep what they were already doing, and the
canon will become legible."

The corrective is structural. Rearranging the canon so that the
order between elements is explicit — even when the order is "these
are simultaneous, by the following discipline" — is the move that
restores the phenomenon to the document.

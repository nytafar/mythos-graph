# Historicism as decomposition

## Failure description

Brand-side production of "our story," "our journey," "our history"
content that performs the sacralisation-of-history move (history-as-
meaningful, the brand's narrative as developmental and significant)
while standing on a metaphysics that has disclaimed any
transhistorical reality. The brand uses the *form* of sacred
history (we came from there, we have been called toward this, we
are on the way) without the *substance* of sacred history (the
calling is real, the destination is real, the meaning is not the
brand's projection).

Eliade's diagnosis: this is the modern philosophical mode he calls
"historicism" — the position that all meaning is historical and
nothing transhistorical is real — and historicism is, in his
reading, a "decomposition product of Christianity." Christianity
sacralised history (the Incarnation made historical time
sanctifiable); historicism kept the historical, dropped the
sanctification, and inherited a metaphysical position that cannot
sustain the meaning-claims it keeps making. The brand-side version
is structurally the same: the brand's "story" matters, the brand's
"history" matters, but the brand cannot say *why* without invoking
the transhistorical claim it has already foreclosed.

The failure is not that brand-history-content is bad rhetorically;
it can be polished and effective. The failure is that the rhetoric
performs a metaphysical claim the brand will not own. StoryBrand-
style "your story matters, the brand's story matters, history is
the medium of meaning" is operating on borrowed metaphysical
capital — capital the brand has not deposited and cannot replenish.
The audience hears the meaning-claim, responds to it, and the
brand collects the response without bearing the weight of the
claim. When the audience asks (implicitly or explicitly) *why* the
history matters, the brand has no answer that is not itself
historicist — *because we are the brand telling the story*. The
circularity collapses the credibility.

## Source's diagnosis

### From [[eliade-sacred-and-profane]]

Eliade names the diagnosis in chapter II at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p155]]–[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p157]],
section "Sacred History, History, Historicism." The argument runs
through Christianity, then Hegel, then twentieth-century
historicism.

The Christian innovation at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p155]]: "for Judaism, time has a
beginning and will have an end. The idea of cyclic time is left
behind. Yahweh no longer manifests himself in cosmic time but in
a historical time, which is irreversible. Each new manifestation
of Yahweh in history is no longer reducible to an earlier
manifestation… Hence the historical event acquires a new
dimension; it becomes a theophany." Christianity extends this:
"since God was incarnated, that is, since he took on a
historically conditioned human existence, history acquires the
possibility of being sanctified… History becomes sacred history
once more — as it was conceived, but in a mythical perspective,
in primitive and archaic religions"
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p156]].

The Hegelian extension at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p156]]: "Hegel takes over the
Judaeo-Christian ideology, and applies it to universal history in
its totality: the universal spirit continually manifests itself in
historical events and manifests itself only in historical events.
Thus the whole of history becomes a theophany; everything that has
happened in history had to happen as it did, because the
universal spirit so willed it." Hegel preserves history-as-
revelation by turning the universal spirit into the actor.

The decomposition diagnosis at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p156]] is the corpus-load-bearing
sentence: "historicism arises as a decomposition product of
Christianity; it accords decisive importance to the historical
event but to the historical event as such, that is, by denying it
any possibility of revealing a transhistorical, soteriological
intent." Historicism keeps the Christian claim that history is
where meaning happens, drops the Christian claim that the meaning
comes from outside history. The result is a position that
maintains the importance of history without grounding the
importance.

The terrifying-modern-time consequence at
[[eliade-sacred-and-profane#^p157]]: "as for the conceptions of
time on which certain historicistic and existentialist philosophies
have insisted, the following observation is not without interest:
although no longer conceived as a circle, time in these modern
philosophies once again wears the terrifying aspect that it wore
in the Indian and Greek philosophies of the eternal return.
Definitively desacralized, time presents itself as a precarious
and evanescent duration, leading irremediably to death." The
historicism that started by sacralising history-as-such has
arrived at history-as-irredeemable-flux. The position is
internally unstable; it cannot sustain its initial meaning-claim
on its own resources.

For brand-mythos: any narrative that uses "our journey,"
"our history," "the meaning of what we are doing" without an
operative cosmology behind those terms is operating in the
historicist register Eliade names. The pressure-test is whether
the brand can articulate *why* its history matters without
collapsing into "because we are telling it." If the answer is
historicist (the story matters because we are constituting our
identity through it), the position is the decomposition Eliade
diagnoses. If the answer points to a transhistorical claim the
brand can stand on (an inherited substrate, a real cosmological
commitment, a genuine paradigm), the position is grounded — and
the language of journey/story/history is operative because it is
backed.

## Corrective

The corrective is the cosmology move. Either ground the brand-
history in something transhistorical the brand can name and stand
on, or stop using brand-history language at the meaning-bearing
weight. Both moves are operative; the failure is the third — using
the language at full meaning-bearing weight without the grounding.

For brands willing to ground: the substrate work is the corrective.
Locate the inheritance the brand draws from (craft tradition, place,
material substrate, historical lineage with named inheritors,
philosophical commitment with named sources). Make the inheritance
explicit. The brand's story then becomes "we inherit *this*, we are
working *this* forward, the meaning of what we do comes from *this*"
— and the historical-narrative language operates because it is
backed by transhistorical claim. This is the engine's reverse-
compression discipline applied to the controlling-idea dimension.

For brands not willing to ground: the corrective is to drop
StoryBrand-inflected meaning-language and write copy that does not
make meaning-claims it cannot back. Functional, benefit, feature
language is honest at this level even when sage-protocol prefers
mythic register; the alternative is the camouflaged-sacred
anti-pattern next door.

## Workshop pressure-test relevance

When canon performs sacralisation-of-history language without
standing on transhistorical commitment, this anti-pattern fires.

**Pressure-test signature.** Canon copy uses "our story," "our
journey," "our history," "the why," "the meaning of," "the
purpose of" at meaning-bearing weight, but the brand cannot
articulate the transhistorical claim that grounds the meaning.
The copy reads as if the brand's history matters; the metaphysics
the copy implies has been foreclosed by the brand's actual
philosophical commitments (or absence of commitments).

**What to look for.** Brand "about" copy that uses
journey/origin/calling/purpose language without grounding it.
"We were called to" / "we set out to" / "we believed there had to
be a better way" — language that performs sacred-history-of-the-
brand without the transhistorical anchor. Founder origin stories
written as biography but read as theophany. Mission statements
that perform meaning-bearing weight ("our purpose is to…") without
the controlling-idea work that would let the purpose-claim stand.

**Diagnosis voice.** "The brand here is making a meaning-claim
that historicism's metaphysics will not bear. The copy reads as
sacred-history; the philosophical position the brand actually
holds is that nothing transhistorical is real. The position is
internally unstable — Eliade calls it Christianity's decomposition
product — and the audience will eventually register the
instability. Either ground the meaning-claim in something the
brand can stand on transhistorically, or write copy that doesn't
ask history to do work the brand has not credentialed history to
do."

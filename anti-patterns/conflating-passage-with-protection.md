# Conflating passage with protection

## Failure description

Reading a single ceremony as if all of its rites served the same
purpose. In any complex passage, multiple distinct ceremonial
streams run in parallel: rites of passage that move the subject from
one defined position to another, rites of protection that defend the
subject (and sometimes the surrounding kin or community) against
specific dangers attendant on the change, sympathetic rites that
operate on associated outcomes (fertility, fortune, ease of delivery),
and propitiatory rites toward specific deities. The streams co-occur
within the same ceremony and often within the same rite-act.

The failure is to read the ceremony as a single intentional pattern
when it is in fact a layered combination. A pregnancy ceremony does
not "have one purpose"; it has several, and they require different
analytic frames. Conflating them collapses the rite-of-passage
phenomenon into "rites in general," and the order-as-meaning-unit
thesis cannot survive the collapse.

## Source's diagnosis

### From [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage]]

van Gennep names the methodological hazard at
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p089]]: "Pregnancy ceremonies, like
those of childbirth, include a great many rites — sympathetic or
contagious, direct or indirect, dynamistic or animistic — whose
purpose is to facilitate delivery and to protect mother and child…
There is no need to deal with them here, and I mention them only to
make it clear that I do not mean to lump them all in the category of
rites of passage. It is often difficult to discern clearly whether a
particular ceremony is a rite of passage, a rite of protection, or
a sympathetic rite (with reference to birthmarks, for example)."

The discipline is restated in the conclusions at
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p378]]: "I do not maintain that all
rites of birth, initiation, etc., are rites of passage only, or that
all peoples have developed characteristic rites of passage for birth,
initiation, and so forth. Funeral ceremonies in particular… may
consist primarily of defensive procedures against the soul of the
deceased and rules of prophylaxis against the contagion of death;
in that case they present only a few aspects of the typical pattern."

van Gennep is careful with the distinction because he is writing
against scholarship that had collapsed it. Frazer and Crawley had
read pregnancy seclusion as "purification" and miscoded it; van
Gennep reads the same practice as multiply-coded — the seclusion is
simultaneously protection (against the dangerous condition of the
mother) and passage (the preliminal separation from the non-pregnant
condition). The two readings are not in competition; both are
operative; the analysis must hold both.

## Corrective

The corrective is multi-stream reading. When analysing a ceremony,
identify the passage stream first (preliminal-liminal-postliminal)
and let it organise the analysis; then identify the protection
stream, sympathetic stream, and propitiatory stream as parallel,
overlapping, sometimes embedded in the same rite-acts. The streams
are not competing interpretations; they are co-present functions.

For the corpus, the corrective is the discipline that a rite's
*position in the passage sequence* is one piece of information; its
*function in the protection sequence* is another; and a single rite
can carry both at once. Reading only one is reading half the
ceremony.

## Workshop pressure-test relevance

When canon mistakes an act of protection for an act of passage — or
the reverse — this anti-pattern fires.

**Pressure-test signature.** Brand work that frames a defensive move
(an apology, a refund, a security feature) as if it produced
transformation. Or work that frames a transformation moment (the
identity-shifting boon) as if it merely protected the customer from
a previously-existing risk. The categories carry different weight in
canon: protection sustains the customer in their current state;
passage moves them.

**What to look for.** Customer-arc documents where a defensive
operation (resolving a billing issue, recovering from a service
interruption, apologising for a missed expectation) is written as if
it were a transformation beat. Conversely, a real transformation
moment described in defensive language ("we will keep you safe
through this change") that masks the passage as if it were merely
protection.

**Diagnosis voice.** "What is described here is a protection rite —
the customer's existing position is being defended. That work is
real, and it matters, but it is not what produces the transformation
the document elsewhere claims. Either the protection has been
mislabelled as passage, or a passage moment is missing from the
sequence and the protection has been pressed into service to fill
the gap. Locate the actual passage; let the protection do its own
job."

The corrective is to keep the streams separate at the level of
analytic vocabulary, even where they are inseparable in the lived
ceremony. Naming the streams is what allows the canon to know what
it is doing.

# Initiation as puberty rite

## Failure description

Anchoring the rite of initiation to the body's physiological event —
menarche, sperm emission, secondary sexual characteristics — and
treating the social transition as if it were the biological event's
ceremonial expression. The failure mistakes the lawful timing of the
ceremony for a calendar that is in fact internal to the body and
indifferent to the social schedule that the ceremony enacts.

The structural error: the body's calendar and the society's calendar
are independent variables. Where they happen to align, initiation
and physical maturation occur close in time, and the alignment
encourages the misreading. Where they diverge — and they reliably
diverge, sometimes by years — the misreading produces an unworkable
account: the rite that does not occur at puberty cannot be a puberty
rite, but the framework requires it to be one. The framework breaks.

For brand work, the failure has a precise translation. Anchoring an
initiation moment to a physiological-equivalent — first purchase,
first signup, first usage, first session — treats the body's act
(the customer doing something) as the ceremony, when in fact the
ceremony is the community's *recognition* that the act has occurred
and that the new member now exists. The signup is not the threshold;
the threshold is when the brand-community treats the new member as
member. Confuse the two and the entire rite is anchored to the wrong
moment.

## Source's diagnosis

### From [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage]]

van Gennep diagnoses Schurtz and Webster directly at
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p138]]: "both authors, imbued with the
notion that initiation coincides with puberty and that this
physiological phenomenon is the point of departure for all such
ceremonies, have launched into unacceptable general theories." Their
inability to escape the puberty frame produced theoretical structures
they then defended by treating divergent cases as deviations.

The empirical refutation runs at
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p140]],
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p141]],
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p144]]: physiological puberty varies
across populations and individuals so widely that "one cannot
conceive of any institution being founded on an element as
undeterminable and as irregular as puberty." The Roman case — most
girls do not menstruate at the legal marriage age of twelve — and
the Parisian case — average physical puberty four years before the
average age of marriage — together demolish the alignment hypothesis.

van Gennep proposes the operative correction at
[[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p144]]: "It is appropriate to
distinguish between *physical puberty* and *social puberty*, just
as we distinguish between *physical kinship* (consanguinity) and
*social kinship*, between *physical maturity* and *social maturity*
(majority)."

The Toda case at [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p148]] gives the
extreme: ritualised pre-pubertal sexual encounters with no marriage
following until years after physiological maturation. The Masai case
at [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p176]] runs the opposite way:
circumcision postponed for years past physical readiness because the
parents lack the funds to host the ceremony. In both cases, the body
is not setting the calendar; the social institution is, and the body
is being asked to wait or to be ready earlier than it would
spontaneously.

The general point at [[van-gennep-rites-of-passage#^p147]]: "I answer
a definite no" to whether initiation coincides with sexual maturity.

## Corrective

The corrective is to keep the social transition's calendar and the
biological event's calendar analytically separate, and to allow them
to align or diverge according to the institution's logic, not the
body's. Initiation rites mark the community's recognition that the
new member exists. The body's events are part of the surrounding
material, but they are neither the trigger nor the calendar.

For brand work: do not anchor the initiation moment to the
customer's body-act (the click, the form-fill, the first usage).
Anchor it to the community's recognition — the moment the brand
treats the customer as member, where treatment is observable in
material consequences (access, voice, role, name). The body-act is
the prerequisite; the recognition is the rite.

## Workshop pressure-test relevance

When canon work locates initiation at the customer's act rather than
at the community's recognition, this anti-pattern fires.

**Pressure-test signature.** Customer-arc documents where the
threshold-crossing beat is timed to a customer action (signup,
purchase, install) rather than to a community-act of welcome
(introduction by name, granting of access, public marking, first
relational encounter). The misalignment shows in the document's
treatment of the post-action period — if the canon treats the
customer as transformed merely because they have acted, the rite
has been collapsed onto the body's calendar.

**What to look for.** Onboarding flows that conclude at the
completion of a setup task. Welcome emails that congratulate the
customer for an action rather than mark the community's reception
of the new member. Boon documents where the transformation is dated
to the customer's purchase rather than to a moment of community-
recognition. "You're in!" written in active voice with the customer
as agent rather than recipient.

**Diagnosis voice.** "The act here is the customer's, and the
ceremony is treating the act as if it were the rite. It is not. The
rite is the brand-community's recognition of the act, and that
recognition has not yet happened. The customer has done the body's
part; the community has not yet done its part. Find or build the
moment of recognition, and the rite will be located where it
actually occurs."

The corrective restores the subject of the verb. Initiation is done
*to* the initiate by the community; it is not done *by* the
initiate. Brand work that treats the customer as the agent of their
own initiation has misunderstood whose ceremony this is.
